title: "The Ontological Error"
date: 2025-11-29
summary: "California SB 331 proposes to let a private committee's symptom checklist define when a citizen loses their freedom. This is a category error."
---

The sound of a magnetic lock engaging on a psychiatric unit is final. It is the sharp, mechanical punctuation mark that separates a citizen with rights from a patient under the state's control. In my 792 hours on the acute floor, I have heard that sound hundreds of times. Standing on the inside, observing the intake process, you realize that this click does not just represent a change in location. It represents a metaphysical shift. The person behind the door has been deemed to have lost their agency.

California Senate Bill 331 proposes to change the rules for that lock. It would define a "mental health disorder" in the Lanterman-Petris-Short Act as any condition listed in the current edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders. To a politician, this looks like a housekeeping measure. To a philosopher, it is a category error. It confuses the map for the territory, and in doing so, it threatens to turn the definition of human liberty over to a private committee that never intended to write a constitution.

The DSM is a masterpiece of descriptive utility. It allows a doctor in Tokyo to communicate with a doctor in Toronto about "Schizophrenia" using a shared vocabulary. But the DSM is nominalist, not realist. It does not describe natural kinds that exist in nature like gold or tuberculosis. It describes practical kinds, clusters of symptoms that tend to appear together. The DSM is theory-neutral. It does not care why you are depressed or look for the biological mechanism. It asks if you have five of these nine symptoms.

SB 331 takes this descriptive tool and treats it as a legal threshold. If a person fits a cluster in the book, they have met the standard for state intervention. But the DSM lists 265 conditions, including Caffeine Withdrawal, Restless Leg Syndrome, and Adjustment Disorder. By tethering the legal definition of mental disorder to this ever-expanding catalog, the state is arguing that descriptions of distress are legally equivalent to states of incapacity. A checklist of behaviors is not the same thing as the loss of the self.

This is where my time on the floor collides with the philosophy of Thomas Fuchs. Fuchs argues that true mental illness is not a software glitch or a wrong thought. It is a fundamental disruption of what he calls "Being-in-the-world," a breakdown of the lived structure of time, space, and the body. I have seen this breakdown. A patient stands in the middle of the hallway, weeping not because they are sad but because they have lost the ability to synthesize time. The future has collapsed into the present. They are not acting irrationally. They are inhabiting a different phenomenological reality where their agency is structurally impossible.

That is the threshold for removing someone's liberty.

But I also see patients who are poor, addicted, or eccentric. They might meet the DSM criteria for Substance Use Disorder or Antisocial Personality Disorder, but they have not lost their Being-in-the-world. They are making rational, autonomous choices within a harsh environment. SB 331 ignores this distinction. By focusing on the checklist rather than the phenomenology, it creates a dragnet that allows the state to detain the struggling subject with the same authority it uses for the dissolved subject.

We rely on the DSM because psychiatry is still a science of observation, not measurement. In other fields of medicine, we have objective limits. We do not detain someone for looking diabetic. We detain them because their blood glucose is 40 mg/dL and they are in a coma. The biomarker proves the biological mechanism has failed. In psychiatry, we do not have those biomarkers. We cannot measure the prediction error in a psychotic brain or the reward function in an addicted brain. We cannot mathematically prove that a person's internal control system is broken. Because we lack these objective tools, we rely on subjective observation. And because subjective observation is flawed, the legal standard for removing someone's freedom must remain extraordinarily high.

If anyone should know this, it is the field's own leadership. In 2013, Dr. Thomas Insel, then director of the National Institute of Mental Health, declared that the NIMH would pivot away from using the DSM for research. His reasoning was precise: the DSM has reliability, meaning doctors agree on the label, but lacks validity, meaning the label does not map to a biological reality. Insel put it simply. Biology never read that book.

Dr. Nassir Ghaemi puts a number on this failure. When the DSM-III was published in 1980, it introduced 292 diagnoses. Only 14 of them had valid scientific evidence. The other 278 were invented by committee consensus to unify the profession. That means the document was 95.2 percent unvalidated at its inception. We are not talking about a rough draft that was later refined through decades of research. Many of those original categories remain, largely unchanged, in the current edition. By codifying these committee compromises into law, SB 331 treats a trade union's negotiated treaty as though it were biological fact.

Ghaemi, a recent president of the Massachusetts Psychiatric Society, has also pointed out that the DSM is the economic engine of the American Psychiatric Association. Royalties from its sales fund a significant portion of the APA's operating budget. By mandating the DSM as the legal standard for civil commitment, the state is not just adopting a flawed diagnostic tool. It is subsidizing a private organization's revenue stream and giving that organization indirect power over the legal boundaries of human freedom.

Proponents of SB 331, including the California State Association of Psychiatrists, argue that we should trust clinicians. They say that excluding diagnoses from the law freezes medical practice, and that no doctor would detain someone for Caffeine Withdrawal unless they were truly gravely disabled. But this argument assumes the system operates in good faith. It does not always.

In September 2024, the New York Times published an investigation into one of the nation's largest psychiatric hospital chains. The investigation described a systematic business model in which patients were lured in with free assessments and then held to maximize insurance payouts. Staff were pressured to exaggerate symptoms to justify continued detention. Quiet patients were documented as "withdrawn." Polite patients who asked to leave were labeled "combative." The goal was not clinical stabilization. It was to extend the stay until the insurance authorization ran out. One executive was quoted as saying that if there were insurance days left, that patient was going to be held.

This is the danger of SB 331 in practice. By defining mental disorder as anything in the DSM, we hand these corporations a massive expansion of their catchment area. If the law allows a facility to detain a citizen for Adjustment Disorder, we are effectively removing the legal guardrails that prevent false imprisonment. When the legal standard is subjective, the tie always goes to the house. And the house always wins.

Science writer John Horgan has called this broader pattern "Neurocentrism," the mistaken belief that we can explain all human behavior by looking at neurons. Despite decades of hype, neuroscience has not produced a single unifying theory of the mind or a single reliable biomarker for mental illness. Yet SB 331 asks us to codify this uncertainty into law, treating the soft descriptions of the DSM with the same legal weight as the hard laws of physics. We are confusing the biological hardware with the social software, assuming that every problem of living is a problem of the brain.

This confusion has consequences measured in lives. A July 2024 study by the Federal Reserve Bank of New York analyzed the causal effects of involuntary hospitalization on the exact population SB 331 aims to capture: marginal patients. The findings were devastating. For these patients, involuntary commitment nearly doubled the probability of dying by suicide or overdose in the months following release. The study found that the disruption to the patient's social stability, their housing, their employment, their relationships, outweighed any clinical benefit of the hospitalization itself. By widening the net to catch these marginal cases, we are not saving them. We are statistically increasing their mortality. We are destroying their social stability to treat a biological condition we have not even validated.

The definition of who gets to be free is the most sacred duty of a democracy. It should be defined by the Constitution and the people, not by a private manual published in Virginia and revised by committees subject to academic trends, pharmaceutical lobbying, and cultural shifts. If California ties its civil liberty laws to the current edition of the DSM, it is outsourcing its sovereignty. It is saying that if a private committee decides next year that Prolonged Grief is a disorder, a citizen in California can lose their freedom for grieving too long. We are confusing a billing manual for a Bill of Rights.

On the floor, amidst the noise and the paper charts, the difference between struggle and sickness is something you can feel. The staff knows it. The patients know it. We do not need a wider net. We need a more precise instrument. Until we have the computational tools to objectively prove that a mind has lost its capacity for agency, and until we can stratify the biological break from the social struggle, we must not allow a book of symptoms to become a weapon of the state.

We must protect the distinction between the map and the territory, because once the lock clicks shut, the territory is the only thing that matters.
