title: "What I'm Learning About Stability"
date: 2025-05-28
summary: "On stress, sleep, and the man at Panera Bread whose life showed me how short the distance is between stability and crisis."
---

They say money does not bring happiness, but that really depends on who you ask and what their life looks like. I have never been the type to tie my happiness to money or material things, but I have always understood how important it is to meet basic needs. Shelter, food, clothing, paying your bills.

In my view, having your basic needs met does not bring happiness so much as it brings less stress. And I say less stress, not no stress, because just getting by still leaves you in a fragile spot. Any unexpected turn can bring back that deep pressure. I believe stress is what directly shapes our moods. Maybe it is more accurate to say money acts as a mood stabilizer by removing some of these pressures rather than by adding joy.

True happiness is personal. When a date recently asked me, "When are you happiest?" my answer came easily. My life has taught me that I find a particular kind of joy and mental quiet when riding my bicycle. It is one of the rare times where my mind seems to completely shut down.

Needing that quiet is a big part of my daily life. I often find it hard to slow my thoughts enough to sleep early. I am frequently up at night working on my computer, reading, or doing simple chores like laundry. My mind is wired to see tasks through. If I start something, or even just think about starting it, I feel compelled to finish it instead of putting it off. This led to a habit of napping during the day to make up for lost sleep, and my sleep scores were never good.

A turning point came earlier this year, thanks to a date. She pointed out that my poor sleep quality would eventually affect my mood. Because I really liked her and did not want my sleep habits to become a problem between us, I genuinely tried to change. I removed electronics from my bedroom first, but that just led to more reading. So I took a stricter approach. I would lie in the dark until sleep came. No negotiating with myself. The effort paid off. I finally got a full 8 hours and 12 minutes of uninterrupted sleep, with a sleep score of 95.

That was a small victory, but it taught me something important. The fix was not complicated. The barrier was behavioral, not medical. I needed someone I cared about to make me take the problem seriously, and then I needed to commit to a simple discipline. Most of the time, the obstacle between us and better health is not a lack of information. It is a lack of structure and support.

That lesson stayed with me when I met a man at Panera Bread a few weeks later. He was an engineering manager who had lost his job in 2023 and had not been able to find a comparable permanent role since. You could feel the weight of his responsibilities the moment he started talking. He has a wife and two young children, five and two, depending on him. His wife is now the family's main earner while he pieces together income from delivery gigs and low-paying short-term IT contracts.

He told me he had used up his 401k and part of an overseas pension just to cover bills, but that money ran out by March 2025. He was four months behind on his mortgage. A first-time late payment had damaged his credit score, and a creditor responded by lowering his credit limit on cards that were already maxed out. He had reached a point where affording gas or paying his phone bill was a daily calculation.

I texted him afterward to ask a direct question: did his experience make him feel sad or stressed? His answer was immediate. Stressed. Not sad. His thoughts were consumed by the challenge of meeting his bills and the cascading consequences for his credit and any future recovery. The stress had taken over every part of his life. He told me he had given up playing soccer, a hobby he once loved. Family outings were no longer possible. They were stuck in a housing trap. They hesitated to sell the house because a foreclosure would destroy his credit, making it impossible to rent a new place. The situation had forced them to change their children's schools.

Here was a person with over fourteen years of tech experience and a U.S. master's degree who consistently gets to the final interview stages with top companies like OpenAI and Anthropic, yet repeatedly falls short of an offer. His skills had not deteriorated. The market had shifted underneath him.

His situation made me pay closer attention to what was happening in the broader economy. I started reading about the impact of AI on the job market, and the picture was sobering. Some companies had adopted what amounted to an AI-first hiring policy, checking whether AI could handle a task before looking for a human to do it. One tech executive said his company had stopped hiring below the mid-level L5 engineer because AI could handle the more junior work. Dario Amodei, the CEO of Anthropic, gave an even sharper warning, saying AI could potentially eliminate half of all entry-level white-collar jobs within five years.

As someone who works with this technology as both a developer and a consumer, I have seen these trends play out firsthand. My own software and consulting business started losing clients who found they could use AI for services we had offered. With revenue declining, I had to part ways with three members of my team. These worries were on my mind earlier this year, and the anxiety they produced was a major reason for my sleep problems.

I also spent time with AI in a different context. I drove over 16,000 miles cross-country in a car with supervised full-self-driving. The car navigated complex traffic scenarios with impressive skill, drawing on its training data. But it was not perfect. It made its first major error at my final destination, hitting a small barricade. On another test, it handled a steep, narrow road well until it met a very sharp turn and simply stopped, forcing me to take over.

Those moments reminded me of a truck driver I once watched at a warehouse job. He could navigate incredibly tight spaces and park perfectly using only his side mirrors, never hitting anything. That natural human ability to read small details, to keep learning, and to connect ideas across different situations is something AI still struggles to replicate.

So my view is not that AI will replace all human jobs. I think it will sharply reduce how many people are needed for many roles. If a team once needed ten engineers, AI might let them do the same work with five. This creates fierce competition for fewer positions. The effects will hit entrepreneurs too. Where you could once build and sell software to companies, those companies can now use their own engineers and AI to build similar tools internally.

But here is what stays with me. The man I met at Panera did everything right. He got the degree. He built the experience. He interviews well enough to reach the final round at some of the most competitive companies in the world. And he still cannot find stable work. His family is unraveling not because of any personal failure but because the economic ground shifted and the safety nets were not there.

I count myself lucky. The conversations with the date I mentioned, who works in the mental health field, were a huge help in getting me to take my own well-being seriously. I also benefit from ongoing therapy and the unwavering support of my family and two closest friends. But seeing firsthand how fragile stability can be, even for someone as capable and hardworking as the man at Panera, reinforced something I keep coming back to. Money is not the same as happiness. But having enough to cover your needs and feel stable removes a deep layer of stress. If that stress is not dealt with, it can make every other part of life feel impossible to manage.

The distance between stability and crisis is shorter than most of us think. And for a lot of people right now, that distance is closing.
