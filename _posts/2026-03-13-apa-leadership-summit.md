I recently attended the 2026 American Psychiatric Association Leadership Summit in Montgomery, Alabama, from March 5 to March 7. This was my first time attending since being accepted into their fellowship program. What started as a conference trip turned into one of the most clarifying experiences of my life.

I applied to this fellowship a day before the deadline. I did not expect to get in. I had no experience in psychology. I was not even a pre-med student at the time. I stumbled on the application by accident while going through a very tough period last year. I was looking into mental health resources, which led me to the APA website to learn what psychiatry actually is and what psychiatrists do. That is where I found the application.

The program has been incredibly helpful in my preparation for medical school. We have a speaker series featuring everyone from psychiatrists to lawyers, study resources including a funded 12-month Kaplan MCAT preparation course, a current medical student mentor, and a practicing psychiatrist mentor. For this trip, the APA generously covered all of our expenses, including flights, hotel, food, transportation, and lunch during the conference days.

By the time I got on the plane to Montgomery, I was not starting from zero. I had already been working as a mental health worker in an acute psychiatric hospital for several months. I had spent hundreds of hours on the floor doing 15-minute rounds, one-to-one observations, admissions, and transports. I had watched patients cycle through medications that did not work. I had seen intake processes built around liability instead of care. I had ridden in the back of an ambulance and seen how an emergency room uses sensors, imaging, and blood work to find a clot that no human eye could detect, and then walked back into a psych unit where we still documented safety rounds on paper. That gap between what medicine can do and what psychiatry actually does had already become the central question of my life.

That question is what led me to start building. I had been developing two projects under Stratification Labs. Psykick is a platform built to relieve the administrative burden that eats psychiatrists alive. The average psychiatrist spends over ten hours a week fighting insurance companies and filling out forms, more than any other specialty. Psykick automates prior authorizations, generates clinical documentation through an AI scribe, defends claims against bogus denials, handles electronic prescribing, and gives providers a dashboard to see exactly where their time goes. The goal is simple: stop making psychiatrists do paperwork and let them treat patients. Psychify is the clinical research side. It uses foundation models to stratify patient biotypes and predict treatment response at day zero, before the months of trial and error that currently define psychiatric prescribing. If Psykick removes the administrative friction, Psychify goes after the diagnostic uncertainty.

Coming into the summit, I knew the problems. What I did not have was confirmation from inside the medical establishment that I was heading in the right direction. I was not sure how traditional doctors would receive someone approaching psychiatry from an engineering background. I was about to find out.

My trip started with a flight from Los Angeles to Charlotte, then a connection on the smallest plane I have ever been on for the final leg to Montgomery. I arrived around midnight, and finding an Uber at the airport at that hour was its own challenge. During the day, downtown Montgomery was strikingly quiet. I barely saw anyone walking around. Locals told me people come out more at night, but it had a completely different energy than any other American city I have visited. It felt like a place built more for reflection than for rush. I would soon understand why.

The theme of the summit was changing landscapes and informed mental health policy. We heard from clinicians, healthcare researchers, and lawyers with health policy degrees across two days of talks, capped by three book signings. What struck me most was how heavily the speakers focused on wealth. There is an undeniable economic dimension to mental health and well-being, and hearing experts lay that out so clearly was fascinating, especially because I had just finished an introductory class on the U.S. healthcare sector. Much of what the speakers discussed matched what I had learned in class. I remember using the mental health parity law as an example of how the government tries to improve healthcare access through policy. Hearing that same concept discussed at the national level by the people actually shaping these policies made the stakes feel completely different.

This conference validated a lot of what I have written about in the past. My aspiration to become a psychiatrist grew significantly during this trip. It confirmed my belief that many of the problems we face in the mental health space are rooted in inadequate social, political, and economic policies, not just clinical shortcomings.

The summit was held at the Elevation Convening Center, a venue built by the Equal Justice Initiative as a companion to the Legacy Sites. It was not a coincidence that we were there. The building itself has a library, a theater, and learning spaces designed to extend the experience of the nearby museum and memorial. So when we stepped outside the conference room to tour The Legacy Sites, the transition felt natural. We observed a moment of silence for Bloody Sunday. The summit's final day, March 7, fell on the 61st anniversary of the 1965 attack on voting rights marchers at the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, just 50 miles west of where we were gathered. Commemorations were happening across the state that same weekend.

But The Legacy Museum made the strongest and most emotional impression on me. Growing up in Africa, I understood the history of European colonization and the devastation it caused across the continent. That was the lens I carried with me. My grievance had always been with the Europeans who carved up Africa, extracted its resources, and left behind borders drawn for their convenience. What I had not fully confronted until walking through that museum was the full machinery of what happened on this side of the Atlantic. It was not that the history was new to me. It was that I had never been forced to sit inside it. Seeing how people were priced alongside animals, described in language designed to increase their value to slave masters. Seeing advertisements for public lynchings where thousands would gather to watch. Learning about the repossession of enslaved people as collateral for loans, the physical maiming, and how all of this was perfectly legal. Even after abolition, the exploitation continued. Families were separated by pure greed, the demand for free labor to produce cotton, sugar, and tobacco, and to build public infrastructure.

Seeing that level of historical trauma and economic exploitation firsthand connected directly to the summit lectures on the social determinants of health. The brutal economic practices of the past laid the groundwork for the wealth gaps and mental health disparities that persist in these communities today. That connection was not abstract anymore. It was on the walls in front of me.

This experience also pushed me to engage in a racial conversation for the first time in the ten years since I moved to this country. I typically stay out of racially charged discussions. Because I was born in Africa and only came to the U.S. a decade ago, I have always felt my experiences were different, even though I am deeply sympathetic. My understanding of racial injustice was shaped by what Europeans did to Africa. The American story, the domestic slave trade, the lynchings, the systems built to maintain control after abolition, all of that was something I knew about but had never truly sat with. Before we entered the museum, a Black American attendee asked me if I had experienced racism. I told him I had not, or maybe I just did not know how to spot it. He said that was interesting because another African student in his class had said the exact same thing. I do not have a neat conclusion to that exchange. But standing in that museum, I felt the weight of a history that connects to mine in ways I am still learning to understand and talk about.

Another major highlight of the summit was the people. I interacted with many medical students, residents, and fellows who were incredibly generous with their time. They walked me through what medical school is actually like, shared their different pathways to getting there, and even passed along free study guides for the MCAT. I have been to tech hackathons and conferences where everyone is fired up about what they are building. This summit had that exact same energy, but for medicine. It was the highest concentration of doctors I have ever been around in my life. Being immersed in that environment raised my aspirations higher than ever. For the first time, the goal did not feel distant. It felt like the next step.

I also gained real clarity on where my engineering background fits. During the summit, I learned the specific difference between computational psychiatry and digital psychiatry. Computational psychiatry is more closely associated with neuroscience and brain research. Digital psychiatry focuses on building clinical applications for use in everyday psychiatric practice. I even met a resident who works in a digital psychiatry clinic, where they collaborate directly with software engineers to clinically label AI inputs and outputs. That was a pivotal moment. Not because it told me something I did not already suspect, but because it gave me the exact language for what I had been doing. I had spent months on the floor watching the gap between what psychiatry measures and what it should measure. I had already started building tools to close that gap. Hearing a resident describe a clinic that does this work daily made it real in a way my own research had not.

Working as a mental health worker was the best decision I made. It gave me the real mental models and lived experiences I need to build tools that actually work in clinical settings. The summit confirmed that.

One of the most valuable conversations I had was about the route to medical school itself. I had been open to attending an osteopathic school. I liked their holistic approach to medicine, though I originally thought that philosophy might be more relevant to neurosurgery than psychiatry. The residents clarified that the type of medical degree, whether MD or DO, does not really matter for matching into psychiatry. But the degree type does matter if I want to pursue a dual MD/PhD. Most DO schools do not offer a combined degree, especially not a PhD in Health Policy. And here is the part that stopped me in my tracks: the residents told me I could potentially go to medical school for free through the MD/PhD route.

That changed my entire calculus. I am now strongly considering a PhD in Health Policy instead of Engineering. I already have the engineering fundamentals, and digital psychiatry is not as engineering-intensive as computational psychiatry. A health policy foundation would let me address the systemic problems I keep running into, the ones that no amount of code can fix on its own. The residents also gave me tactical advice I would not have found on my own, like understanding the real difference between schools with strict pass/fail grading versus those that grade pass/fail with honors. These are the kinds of details that shape an application strategy, and I would not have known to ask about them without these conversations.

We also talked about life outside the hospital. I met two residents who are married, and they explained something I had never considered: the unspoken timeline of personal life in medicine. They told me the sweet spot for getting married is the fourth year of medical school, right before residency begins. The best time to have children, they said, is during residency, because that is when you actually get protected parental leave. What fascinated me was not the specifics of the timeline, but what it revealed about the profession. Even the most personal decisions in a doctor's life are shaped by institutional structures and policies. It was another version of the same lesson the entire summit kept teaching me. Policy is not just political. It is personal.

For me, I am not pressed to get married right now, and I am not sure I want kids. But understanding how these systems work gave me a much clearer picture of what the next decade of my life could look like, regardless of the personal choices I make.

On the day of my departure, I got a firsthand lesson in exactly why policy matters. My original flight was scheduled to leave around 1:00 PM, but the airline canceled it, citing severe weather in connecting cities like Dallas. When the agent who booked my flight through the APA told me there was nothing that could be done to get me home that day, I started looking into it myself. Flights were still departing from Dallas that afternoon. When I checked the actual weather conditions there, they did not look severe at all.

At the airport, I asked the airline customer service agent if they would cover my hotel cost since the cancellation was on their end. He said no. I pushed back and referenced the Biden administration's automatic refund rule, which requires airlines to compensate passengers when flights are canceled or significantly changed. I also pointed out that flights were still leaving from Dallas, which raised the question of whether this was truly a weather cancellation or an operational decision the airline was attributing to weather to avoid its obligations. As soon as I started citing specific policy, his tone shifted. He told me he was going to make sure I flew out that night. I got on a standby list and boarded a 5:56 PM flight home. When we got to Dallas, the skies were mostly calm with only minor turbulence in the air.

What stayed with me was not just the outcome but the dynamic. The moment I demonstrated that I knew my rights as a passenger, the conversation changed completely. The cancellation that was supposedly impossible to resolve got resolved in a few hours. That interaction crystallized the entire summit for me. Policy does not just exist on paper. It works when people know about it and are willing to use it. But it also fails when people do not know it exists, which is exactly the problem with so many of the mental health policies we discussed at the conference.

This trip completely clarified my next steps. Before attending, I was not sure how my tech background would be received by traditional doctors. Instead of skepticism, I found support. People encouraged me to keep building and told me the medical field desperately needs these skills right now. The problems I had been writing about for months, the diagnostic uncertainty, the administrative waste, the policies that sound good on paper but lack enforcement, those same problems were being discussed at the national level by the people with the power to change them.

Moving forward, I plan to focus on policy research right alongside technology development. The truth is, a tool like Psykick would not even be needed if medical practice was not buried under administrative burdens created by poor policies. Those policies distort everything, right down to the behavior of consumers and insurance companies. On the clinical side, Psychify's mission to end trial-and-error psychiatry through biotype stratification felt more urgent after hearing speaker after speaker describe the same gaps I had seen on the floor. The technology and the policy have to move together.

I left Alabama knowing exactly what I want to do, knowing what I need to build, and knowing that there is a community of people ready to support me in doing it.
