I just finished an introductory course on the U.S. healthcare sector. For months, I had been writing about what I observed as a mental health worker, the administrative chaos, the paper rounds, the insurance-driven discharge timelines, the gap between what patients need and what the system provides. I understood these problems from the floor. Now I was learning the policy architecture that created them.

What surprised me was not how broken the system is. I already knew that. What surprised me was how precisely the incentives are designed to produce exactly the outcomes I see every night.

The foundation of U.S. healthcare billing is a model called Fee-for-Service. Under FFS, providers are paid for each service performed. Every visit, every procedure, every test generates a charge. The flaw is structural: FFS rewards the quantity of services rather than the quality of care. Studies confirm the consequence. Adults in the U.S. receive the generally accepted standard of care only about 55 percent of the time.[^1]

This incentive structure explains something I noticed early in my time on the floor but did not have the language for. Psychiatrists see patients for minutes. Not because they do not care, but because the payment model punishes longer visits. If a psychiatrist tries to spend more time with a patient, the practice loses money. The fee per visit is too low to support it. The result is that the most important conversations, the ones that might actually change a diagnosis or a treatment plan, get compressed into slots designed for throughput. Patients wait days to see a psychiatrist and then get a few minutes. I wrote about this frustration months ago. Now I understand the billing code that causes it.

FFS also has no payment for many of the things that matter most in psychiatric care. There are traditionally no fees for phone calls with patients, for emails, for the hours nurses spend coordinating care and prepping discharge paperwork. Because providers are not paid for this work, it gets squeezed into the margins. I watch our nurses buried at the computer every shift, managing compliance documentation, intake prep, discharge paperwork, flagging what doctors need to sign. In my opinion, much of this workload is optimized for compliance and insurance reimbursement rather than patient care, though I stand to be corrected. What I am not wrong about is that it leaves almost no time for the human interaction that patients are desperate for.

The billing system runs on codes. ICD codes classify the diagnosis. CPT codes describe the procedure. These codes determine how much facilities get paid. For inpatient services, hospitals receive a fixed amount per Diagnostic-Related Group. For outpatient services, the payment is based on Ambulatory Payment Classification. The entire system depends on translating a patient's suffering into a billing category.

This coded system creates its own battles. The most visible one on the psychiatric floor is prior authorization. Prior authorization is the insurer's decision that a service or medication is medically necessary before they will cover it. According to the AMA's most recent physician survey, 84 percent of physicians report that the number of prior authorizations required has increased over the last five years, for both prescription medications and medical services.[^2] I have seen what this looks like in practice. The patient I wrote about in an earlier essay, the one who eventually transferred from the ICU to the DDMI unit and improved, waited nearly a month for the regional center to clear the transfer. The unit that would help was in the same building. The paperwork was the barrier.

Prior authorization is supposed to control costs. In practice, it delays care, burns clinical hours on administrative appeals, and gives insurers a mechanism to deny coverage for treatments that providers have already determined are necessary. This is the exact problem Psykick is built to solve. Automating prior auth requests, tracking approvals, and generating appeals when claims are denied is not a convenience feature. It is a clinical intervention. Every hour a nurse spends arguing with an insurance company is an hour not spent with a patient.

Here is where California law becomes important. California's Mental Health Parity Act, significantly strengthened by Senate Bill 855 in 2020, requires all state-regulated health plans to provide full coverage for the treatment of all mental health conditions and substance use disorders listed in the DSM or the ICD.[^3] Before 2021, the law only covered nine specific severe mental illnesses, including schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, major depression, and obsessive-compulsive disorder. After SB 855, the mandate expanded to cover the full range of diagnoses.

When I look at our patient population, most of them have conditions that fall squarely under this mandate. Depression. Psychosis. Bipolar disorder. Substance use disorders. The law says their treatment must be covered on parity with medical and surgical care. That means equal copays, equal access, no extra prior authorization hurdles that would not exist for a physical health condition.

But parity on paper is not parity in practice. Insurers are required to comply, but enforcement has been slow and uneven. Health plans have a long history of finding ways to limit mental health coverage even when the law says they cannot. They impose utilization review standards that are stricter for behavioral health than for medical care. They maintain provider networks that look adequate on paper but where a significant percentage of listed providers are unavailable when patients actually try to schedule appointments. The law says equal treatment. The floor says otherwise.

This enforcement gap connects directly to a broader pattern in U.S. healthcare policy. The government passes laws that sound good but builds weak mechanisms to enforce them. Hospital Price Transparency is another example. CMS requires hospitals to publish their prices in machine-readable files so consumers can compare costs.[^4] The mandate is clear. But compliance has been inconsistent, and CMS has acknowledged problems with the accuracy and completeness of the data hospitals report. We have a transparency law that does not yet produce transparency.

On the other side of the ledger, insurers engage in practices like downcoding, where they reduce the payment level of a claim by reclassifying the CPT code. The AMA has publicly opposed these practices, arguing that reducing payment based solely on the diagnosis code is inappropriate because the final diagnosis alone does not determine the complexity of the encounter.[^6] The provider does the work. The insurer retroactively decides it was worth less. The provider then has to spend time appealing for correct payment, time that could have been spent with patients.

All of this sits underneath the most recent policy earthquake: the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed into law on July 4, 2025. The OBBBA made sweeping changes to Medicaid and the ACA marketplace. It imposed work requirements for many adults in Medicaid expansion states. If you do not meet 80 hours a month of work or community service, you can lose your Medicaid and become ineligible for ACA premium tax credits. The Congressional Budget Office projected these changes could leave 16 million more people uninsured by 2034.[^5]

The bill also requires states to redetermine Medicaid eligibility every six months instead of annually, tightens eligibility for refugees and asylum seekers, shortens retroactive coverage periods, and imposes new cost-sharing requirements on the expansion population. On the ACA side, the enhanced premium tax credits that had improved affordability are scheduled to expire, and new verification requirements effectively end automatic re-enrollment for subsidized plans.

I do not know how many of my patients are on Medicaid. I am a mental health worker, not a billing administrator. But I know the population I serve. Many of them are homeless. Many of them cycle in and out of the hospital. Many of them have no family support, no stable housing, no job. These are the people Medicaid was designed to cover. If the OBBBA's changes remove even a fraction of them from coverage, the consequences will not be abstract. They will show up on my floor. They will show up as patients who cannot access follow-up care after discharge, who cannot get their medications, who return to the hospital because the system that was supposed to catch them on the outside no longer exists.

The long-term policy goal is to move away from FFS entirely toward Value-Based Care, where providers are rewarded for outcomes rather than volume. Models like Accountable Care Organizations, bundled payments, and capitation are designed to align financial incentives with actual patient health. Under capitation, for example, a provider receives a set amount per patient and has the flexibility to spend it on things FFS does not reimburse, like care coordination, patient education, and non-medical supports like transportation.

This shift is essential. But it is slow and it is uneven. When Value-Based Care succeeds in reducing expensive services like hospitalizations, hospitals receive less revenue. The payment models have to account for this, and the spending targets they set are often based on historical data that cannot predict new diseases, new drugs, or workforce shortages. The transition is happening, but it is happening while the old system is still running, and the patients on my floor are living inside the friction between the two.

I started this essay by saying that what surprised me in the healthcare course was not how broken the system is, but how precisely the incentives produce the brokenness I see. Fee-for-Service creates short visits and administrative overload. Prior authorization creates delays and denials. Parity laws exist but lack enforcement. Transparency mandates exist but lack compliance. And now the OBBBA is pulling coverage away from the people who need it most.

These are not separate problems. They are one system producing predictable outcomes. The floor is where those outcomes land. The patients do not know about CPT codes or DRGs or the OBBBA's Medicaid redetermination schedule. They know they cannot see a psychiatrist for more than a few minutes. They know they are waiting weeks for a transfer that should take days. They know that when they leave, there may be nothing on the other side.

Policy is not abstract. It is the reason the nurse is at the computer instead of at the bedside. It is the reason the patient waits. It is the architecture underneath everything I have written about for the past year.
