## The Problem

Psychiatry is broken in three specific ways.

The first is diagnostic. Psychiatry is one of the few areas of medicine that lacks objective biomarkers for diagnosis and treatment. An ER doctor can order an ultrasound, a CT scan, or a blood panel and know within hours what is wrong with a patient. A psychiatrist cannot. Diagnosis depends on symptoms, self-reporting, and clinical observation. Symptoms overlap. Self-reporting is unreliable. The result is a system built on educated guessing, where half of adults with depression do not improve on their first medication and treatment becomes a months-long cycle of trial and error. If you want to understand this gap viscerally, go shadow an ER doctor for a shift and then shadow a psychiatrist in an acute hospital. The difference will stay with you.

The second is administrative. Psychiatrists spend over ten hours a week on paperwork, more than any other medical specialty. Prior authorizations, insurance appeals, clinical documentation, claims disputes. These burdens are manufactured by the way insurance companies reimburse behavioral health treatments. They eat the hours that should be spent with patients.

The third is political. There are policies on the books that should fix this. The Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act requires insurers to cover behavioral health the same way they cover medical care. But the law lacks real enforcement mechanisms. So the administrative burden persists, insurance companies continue to deny claims at disproportionate rates, and psychiatrists continue to drown in paperwork despite a federal law that says they should not have to.

These problems are known. Organizations like the National Institute of Mental Health, the American Psychiatric Association, and policymakers at every level of government have tried to address them. We are still not where we should be. I do not claim to have all the answers, and I do not think we will change this field overnight. But I believe we can go further than most because we sit at an intersection that almost nobody occupies: computation, policy, and direct clinical experience.

## What We Believe

At Stratification Labs, we believe the core failure of modern psychiatry is that it treats a wildly diverse patient population as though their problems all have the same origin. They do not.

Some patients are on our floors because they are homeless, hungry, or grieving. Their distress is real but it is not a brain disease. It is a response to social collapse. Some patients are there because of substance use that mimics or masks a psychiatric condition. And some patients have genuine neurobiological disorders that require medical intervention.

These are three fundamentally different problems. Treating them with the same diagnostic framework and the same tools is why the system fails. You cannot medicate someone out of poverty. You cannot talk someone out of a dopamine dysfunction. And you cannot restrain someone out of a withdrawal syndrome.

We believe the path forward is stratification. Social stratification. Chemical abuse stratification. Biological stratification. Sort the problems before you try to solve them. Build tools precise enough to tell the difference. And design systems that route each patient to the intervention that actually matches their condition.

## What We Build

Stratification Labs is the company. Psykick and Psychify are the products.

Psykick attacks the administrative burden. It automates prior authorizations, generates clinical documentation through an AI scribe, defends claims against insurance denials, handles electronic prescribing, and gives providers a time analytics dashboard to see exactly where their hours go. The goal is to give psychiatrists back the time the system has stolen from patient care. Most AI scribes just listen. Psykick acts. It unbundles the EMR to handle reception, prescriptions, and audits so clinicians can stop doing paperwork and start treating patients.

Psychify attacks the diagnostic uncertainty. It uses foundation models to stratify patient biotypes and predict treatment response at day zero, before the months of guessing that currently define psychiatric prescribing. If Psykick removes the administrative friction, Psychify goes after the clinical guesswork.

Long term, we want to build medical psychiatric technologies, hardware and software, the way the ER uses sensors and imaging to aid diagnosis. An ER doctor will not order a CT scan for a headache unless the symptoms are severe enough to warrant it. Now imagine trying to use a CT scan to diagnose a headache caused by stress, lack of sleep, dehydration, or skipping meals. That is the precision gap we are working to close. We want to build the instruments that can tell the difference.

## What We Expect

If you want to work at Stratification Labs, there is a process. It is not conventional. It is designed to make sure you understand the problem before you try to build solutions for it.

**Step 1: Read.** Go through the materials listed at the bottom of this page. Then write a two-page essay on what went through your mind while reading them. Not a summary. A reaction. What surprised you. What frustrated you. What you disagree with. What you want to know more about.

**Step 2: Work the floor.** Apply as a per diem mental health worker or unlicensed psychiatric technician at a partner psychiatric hospital. Work for one week. Then write about your experience. What did you see? What did you not expect? What would you change?

This requirement is not optional. I spent hundreds of hours on the floor before I built anything, and it changed what I built and how I built it. You will not understand the problem from a whiteboard. You have to stand in the hallway at 3 AM when a patient is in crisis and feel the weight of a system that is supposed to help them and is failing.

We want people with backgrounds in computer science, artificial intelligence, statistics, machine learning, or physics. We also want people with clinical experience: psychiatrists, nurses, physician assistants. If you come from the clinical side, we will teach you the tech. If you come from the tech side, the floor will teach you the medicine. Either way, you will leave with an understanding of how to apply computational skills to mental health that goes far beyond biology and chemistry.

**The requirement that everyone work on the floor is the foundation of how we think.** We call it limit thinking. Instead of building from assumptions, you build from the boundaries of what is actually possible given the constraints of the real system. You start with the patient. You start with the floor. You start with what is broken. And then you build from there.

I recommend reading the Asimov Press article on limit thinking linked below. It captures the approach better than I can summarize here.

## The Stakes

The psychiatric services industry is worth nearly $300 billion. Almost a billion people globally carry a psychiatric diagnosis. In the U.S. alone, the number is 80 million. The NIMH spends over $45 billion on research annually and fights every year for budget increases.

The toll of psychiatric illness is measured in death and disability. It would be good to reduce that suffering. It is also acceptable to hope we build a sustainable business while doing it. Profit is not our only motivation, but we are not pretending we do not want Stratification Labs to survive and grow. The work does not matter if it cannot sustain itself.
