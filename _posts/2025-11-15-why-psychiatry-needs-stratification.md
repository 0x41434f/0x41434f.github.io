title: "Why Psychiatry Needs Stratification"
date: 2025-11-15
summary: "After 62 days on the floor, I thought psychiatry was fake. Then I found a hospital that was already doing it right."
---

I had a moment a few weeks ago where I thought psychiatry was fake. Not the suffering. The suffering is real. But the system we have built to address it. After 62 days of working as a mental health worker, I was watching patients get diagnosed, watching medications fail, watching the constant cycle of switching drugs or increasing dosages, and none of it seemed credible. I started trying to understand why insurance denies so many behavioral health claims, why hospital policies feel so rigid, why so much of what I was seeing on the floor did not match what the textbooks said it should look like.

I was becoming heartbroken. I had come to this field full of optimism that I could use my computational skills to help. Now I was questioning the entire foundation.

So I went to the source. I read the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders from cover to cover. Then I read the ICD-11. Then I started looking into the history of psychiatry and how the DSM was actually developed. What I found did not reassure me. The history of psychiatry is barbaric. The development of the DSM itself is problematic and not particularly scientific. It helps explain why there have not been many breakthroughs in the field for a long time. The system is built on a shaky foundation.

When I compared the DSM labels to the actual behaviors of the patients I was observing on the floor, they did not line up in most cases. The patient's attitude, their culture, their entire life context seemed to tell a different story than the diagnosis in their chart. I kept seeing a gap between the label and the person.

This is where my background matters, and it is not the background you might expect. In high school, I was an art and humanities student. I fought my placement in the science track because I wanted to study law and political economy. I loved debating. I was analytical in a way that leaned toward systems and arguments, not equations. I looked into the Philosophy, Politics, and Economy program at Oxford. I never got to study that. I came to the U.S. and studied computer science instead. But that early wiring never left. It is why I think I see the floor differently than most people in my role.

When I started working the night shift, I found myself constantly asking questions and looking for answers while I observed patients. I started scribbling notes on puzzle scratch papers. Then I bought a small notebook. I realized I was doing philosophy and psychology simultaneously, trying to understand not just what was happening to these patients but why the system responded to them the way it did.

I know I am not a doctor or a nurse practitioner. But I spend more time with the patients than either of them, thanks to the 15-minute rounds and the one-to-ones. My job is literally to observe and record behavior. From that position, I see a disconnect that I believe the people making diagnoses from brief encounters cannot always see.

Mental illness is highly cultural. I see people admitted for behaviors that, back home, would just get them called "stubborn" and never placed in psychiatric care. The baseline for what counts as mental illness shifts depending on where you are from. More than that, I hold a strong belief that mental illness is in large part driven by the absence of strong social support and the state of our socioeconomic system. When I look at our patients, I do not see a uniform population with brain disorders. I see people whose problems have wildly different origins being funneled into the same diagnostic framework and treated with the same tools.

This is why medical psychiatry on its own feels so limited. The only things that feel genuinely medical on our unit are the medications, the vitals, and the blood and urine draws, which mostly exist to rule out pregnancy or other physical conditions during admission. Everything else is observation and conversation. Yet we treat the entire population as though the answer is pharmaceutical.

This led me to a hypothesis that I kept refining in my notebook. We need stratification. Not one kind. Three kinds.

Social stratification of mental illness. Many of our patients are homeless, hungry, grieving, or trapped in poverty. Their distress is real, but it is not a brain disease. It is a response to impossible conditions. These patients need housing, case management, job support, and community. Medicating them without addressing the social collapse is like putting a cast on a leg that is not broken.

Chemical abuse stratification of mental illness. Substance use disorders overlap with and mimic almost every psychiatric diagnosis. A patient in methamphetamine psychosis looks a lot like a patient with schizophrenia. But the causes are different, the trajectories are different, and the treatments should be different. Lumping them together muddies the data and leads to interventions that miss the actual problem.

Biological stratification of mental illness. For the patients whose conditions are genuinely rooted in neurobiology, in the hardware of the brain, we need objective tools. Biomarkers, imaging, computational models. This is where precision psychiatry and computational psychiatry come in. But this layer should be the last one we reach, not the first one we assume.

My observations were not just abstract. I saw the consequences of failing to stratify play out in real time. A patient was having an episode, and the staff were preparing to call a code grey. I decided to try to de-escalate first. I asked the patient why they thought they were having an episode. They told me it always happens before their menstrual cycle.

I told the charge nurse. The nurse said that was not correct.

But I knew it was a real phenomenon. Not because I am a clinician, but because I had read the DSM-5 from cover to cover, and Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder is in it. It is also a recognized category in the ICD-11. There is an entire organization, the International Association for Premenstrual Disorders, dedicated to it. They even have a free self-screening tool. The patient was telling us exactly what was wrong, and the system dismissed it.

I could not push back. It was outside my scope of practice. But that moment crystallized the problem. The patient had given us the answer. We did not listen because the answer did not fit the workflow.

Another patient always cries when they are disoriented. The chart says "delirium." But I watch them, and I wonder what is actually happening in their brain when they cry. Is the crying itself a signal? Could tears tell us something diagnostic that we are not measuring? We label the behavior and move on. We do not ask what the behavior means.

A few weeks after I started forming this hypothesis, I interviewed for an unlicensed psychiatric technician position at a nonprofit hospital. What I heard in that interview changed my perspective. This facility was already doing stratification. Their patient population was primarily people considered gravely disabled and those with substance abuse issues. They tried not to admit patients with developmental disabilities or those who were primarily a danger to self and others. Not because they would turn them away, but because their focus was specific. They even had a residential facility to discharge patients to while they searched for longer-term placement in the community. Their psych beds were funded by the county and the state, but they were not a county hospital.

What impressed me most was how they handled restraints. No prone positions. They mostly used chairs, bringing the chair to wherever the patient was having an episode. It felt more humane than anything I had seen at my hospital.

That interview was a turning point. It proved that the hypothesis I had been building in my notebook was not just a theory. It was a working model. Somewhere, someone had already figured out that you cannot treat every psychiatric patient the same way. You have to sort the problems before you can solve them.

I believe neuroscience is our most objective path forward for the biological layer, alongside the biopsychosocial framework, since we cannot reduce everything about mental illness to the brain alone. This is where my path comes together. Working as a psychiatric technician gives me more stories, more observations, and more time with patients. But it does not pay enough to be a career, and it does not give me the authority to act on what I see. I could become a psychiatric nurse practitioner, but the training for doctors and nurses is fundamentally different. In some ways, I am already doing a version of residency as a technician, just without the legal power to diagnose, treat, or prescribe. And I lack the medical knowledge I need, especially neuroanatomy.

I need to go to medical school. I do not just want to be a clinician. I want to contribute to scientific research and build psychiatric technology tools. The technician role showed me the problems. Medical school will give me the knowledge and the authority to solve them.

That nonprofit hospital showed me that better systems are possible. My first step in building them is to use the computational skills I already have. I am building a tool to scrape and analyze patient reviews of psychiatric hospitals across California. I cannot work at every facility, so this is a way to gather data at scale and see whether the patterns I observe at my hospital hold up elsewhere.

I am no longer just questioning the field. I am researching how to fix it.
