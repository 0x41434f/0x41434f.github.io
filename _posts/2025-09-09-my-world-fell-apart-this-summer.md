This year did not go the way I expected. I began it with hope and discipline, taking part in a forty-day prayer and fasting program at my church. I believed it would be my best year. Instead, by summer, the life I had built fell apart. Several of my worst fears happened one after another, and every backup plan I had failed. What surprised me was not the collapse itself, but my response. I felt calm, even as everything was breaking.

I was in a race against time. I wanted to build and successfully exit a software company before Artificial General Intelligence changed everything. My team and I used the newest AI tools to build products that impressed people. We got meetings and praise, but not enough paying customers. I do not think it was because I lacked skill. I can design products and talk to customers. The real issue was that I did not have a strong network to sell into big companies, and I always delayed talking to investors, waiting until I had a proven product first. Looking back, I see that habit cost me time I did not have.

When things started going wrong, I tried every backup plan I had. Nothing worked. Within days, several of my deepest fears became real. I felt numb. The foundation of the life I knew cracked all at once. For the first time, I felt complete surrender. There was no immediate way out.

But I did not fall apart. I became calm. My blood pressure during that period never went above 108/62, the lowest reading of my adult life, even while everything I had built was breaking down. My friend J always told me I did not know how to ask for help, and he was right. I leaned on my therapist. My family prayed for me. But I still had one question that would not leave my mind: why was I so calm while my world was collapsing?

That question is what pulled me into mental health. I did not arrive gently. I went searching for an answer and found a field full of open questions instead. I read widely about psychology and psychiatry and kept running into the same problem. Therapy helps, but it has limits. In most areas of medicine, doctors rely on objective tests. Blood work, imaging scans, lab results. If your blood glucose is 40 mg/dL and you are in a coma, there is no ambiguity. The body gives us numbers. Psychiatry is different. Diagnosis depends mostly on symptoms and what patients report. But symptoms overlap, and self-reporting can be unreliable. Without objective markers, psychiatry is in a place similar to where the rest of medicine was before the discovery of germs.

This uncertainty runs deep. The DSM, the manual used to classify mental disorders, was never designed to map onto biological reality. When the DSM-III was published in 1980, it introduced 292 diagnoses. The labels gave doctors a shared language, but the science underneath was thin. Half of adults with depression do not improve on antidepressants or antipsychotics.[^1] The last major breakthrough in psychiatric medication was Clozapine in the 1980s. Development of new drugs has mostly stalled because the bar is brutal. Unlike cancer drugs, which are tested against existing treatments, psychiatric drugs must be tested against a placebo. That roughly doubles the cost and lowers the chance of success. On average, it takes 13 years and costs $1 billion to bring a psychiatric drug to market, compared with 5 years and $300 million for cancer.[^2] The incentives are broken, so the pipeline has dried up.

This made me curious about new ways forward. I found a field called computational psychiatry. It applies math, data, and computer models to study how the brain works when it is healthy and when it is not. It connects to a larger movement called precision psychiatry, which aims to improve diagnosis and treatment by combining genetic tests, brain scans, and molecular data with the behavioral and symptom-based measures we already use. If done well, this could give psychiatry the kind of objective foundation that other areas of medicine already have.

But I also saw the limits. Many AI startups had promised to transform drug discovery and failed. The problem is not only the math. We still know very little about our own biology. Without enough reliable data, even the best models cannot perform well. Under pressure, some companies chased easy targets, producing drugs that were no better than what already existed. That taught me something important. Ambition in this space has to be cautious and grounded in reality, not hype.

So I decided to ground myself. Instead of only building from behind a screen, I stepped into the field directly. I now work part-time as a mental health worker while still building in tech. This gives me a close look at the daily struggles patients face and the challenges clinicians deal with. My goal is to create AI tools that support psychiatry, not replace it. I want to build computational methods that help us better understand how the brain is organized and how it changes during illness.

The deeper I looked, the more I saw how far the problem extends beyond the clinic. Mental health problems cost the global economy $1 trillion each year and result in the loss of 12 billion working days.[^3] Cases of depression rose 25 percent during the pandemic, and the numbers have not returned to normal. Companies claim to support mental health, but one in three workers say they would not tell their boss about a mental health need. Half of those who do share report facing discrimination. Employee assistance programs exist, but most people never use them. Real change happens when managers are trained to listen and make workloads manageable, not when companies issue press releases.

The problem is sharper in medicine itself. In 2023, 45 percent of physicians reported burnout.[^4] Many avoid seeking care because licensing and credentialing forms ask about past treatment. Even though the AMA has pushed for those questions to focus only on current impairment, the fear still keeps doctors silent.

Pilots face a similar trap. Their mental health is closely monitored, but disclosure can end careers. One pilot said that therapy no longer felt safe after the FAA used his therapy notes against him. Another said the need to appear perfect on paper kept him from seeking help at all. When the people we trust with our safety cannot get help without losing their livelihoods, something is structurally wrong.

In many countries, the gap between demand and resources is even wider. Low-income countries often have fewer than two mental health workers for every 100,000 people. But task-sharing, where trained non-specialists provide care, has shown real results. One study found that patients were two and a half times more likely to recover when treated by lay workers from their own community than when treated by a psychiatrist.[^5] The Trieste model in Italy is a striking example. After closing its asylums, the city invested in local community services. Care is open to all without a doctor's referral. The focus is on teamwork and treating people as citizens with rights, not just patients with disorders. Franco Basaglia, the pioneer of this model, said the goal was to "put the illness in parentheses" so that the person, not the diagnosis, is what we see first.[^6]

My own collapse forced me to look at paths I had never considered. Medical school. A PhD in psychology. Nursing school. These paths are long, but they offer the clinical depth I need to do this work properly. The future of psychiatry has to combine both science and human care. Precision medicine will add to, not replace, the empathy and judgment that good psychiatrists already bring.

This summer, my worst fears happened. In the middle of it all, I found a calm I could not explain, and that calm sent me looking for answers in a field that does not have enough of them yet. My ambition is no longer only about building with technology. It is also about healing with people. I began this year with faith, and I carry that same faith into this mission: to help give mental health what it has lacked for too long.
