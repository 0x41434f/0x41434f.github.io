title: "792 Hours in Acute Psychiatric Hospital"
date: 2025-11-01
summary: "After 33 days on the floor, the systems failures are no longer abstract. We are managing risk. We are not yet healing people."

It has been 33 days, or 792 hours, since I began working as a mental health worker in an acute psychiatric hospital. I have floated to different units and worked with the entire demographic spectrum: adolescents, youth, adults, geriatrics, and patients with dual diagnoses of mental health and developmental disabilities. In that time, I have participated in nearly twenty admissions.

Most of these happen late at night or in the early morning hours before 6:00 AM. I am still unsure if intake procedures differ during the day shift. Since my day job clashes with those hours, I have not been able to verify it yet. I am planning to apply to another hospital for per diem weekend shifts just to see how intake is done elsewhere before I take my own observations as universal truth.

What I have seen so far has been eye-opening. A dedicated admissions team handles all referrals. These come from everywhere: emergency rooms, group homes, other hospitals, police, fire, paramedics, and voluntary walk-ins. The team adds the patient's name to the electronic health records. Sometimes they appear on our unit's list before they have even arrived physically.

When the patient arrives, the process is a clinical drill. We take vitals. We get the transfer details from police or paramedics. We check them in, and the mental health workers split tasks. One inventories belongings and sorts cash from contraband. The other joins a nurse for the skin check, which requires asking the patient to cough, squat, and then patting them down for hidden items. We give them a unit gown and the choice of our socks or their own shoes without the laces. We document any existing marks, sometimes with a photograph, so there are no liability questions later.

During this entire process, the nurse asks questions about allergies, medications, and history. Then the patient is offered food and taken to a room.

What is striking is who is not there.

There is no social worker. There is no psychiatrist. The questions are not therapeutic. They are administrative. A doctor and social worker are assigned only after the intake is complete. The nurse leaves a voice note for the doctor, who will not see the patient until morning rounds. The patient's first contact with us is purely about risk management. In California, a hold requires the patient to be a danger to self, a danger to others, or gravely disabled. The intake is built around proving one of those criteria, not around understanding the person.

The result is a generic treatment plan. The goals are always the same. Patient will comply with meds. Patient will not harm self or others. Patient will participate in activities. The nursing diagnoses are just as broad: alteration in mood, depressed mood, altered thought process. These could describe almost anyone on the unit. They describe almost no one specifically.

This matters because it shapes everything that follows. Because we are not asking the right questions at intake, we assign rooms based on random bed availability. These rooms hold three patients at a time. I have seen patients unable to sleep, terrified of their roommates, constantly begging for a room change. We create the friction and then blame the patient when they react to it.

I cannot help but map my own recent history onto this system. When I was going through my own crisis this summer, I had no thoughts of harming myself or others. I was simply terrified that I had not met my personal goals. I wanted to exit my company, retain my customers, grow my business, and not feel like I would be permanently beaten into the underclass because every means of value creation I knew felt like it was eroding. If I had walked in voluntarily, what good would it have done to put me in a room with someone in active psychosis? Taking medications would not have solved my problems. I would not have survived in that environment. Many of our patients are in similar situations. They are homeless, hungry, or grieving. They need a robust social safety net, not just antipsychotics. I would put those patients in a dedicated space with a treatment plan focused on resources and support.

The inefficiency extends far beyond intake. I recently learned that the hospital only adopted an Electronic Health Record system this year. We operate in a fragile hybrid state. Medications are administered using a computerized Medication Administration Record, which is a necessary safety guardrail. But we still document our 15-minute safety rounds on paper.

The engineer in me sees the liability immediately. Paper rounds rely entirely on the honor system. There is no timestamp validation. Rounds can be backfilled or signed in bulk. We manually calculate total sleep hours at the end of the shift, a basic calculation a system could perform instantly. Instead, we rely on tired staff to do the math at the end of a 12-hour overnight, making the data prone to error.

The paper trail grows with our high-observation patients. We use paper for one-to-one observation logs and for ABC charts, which track the antecedent, behavior, and consequence of specific incidents like head-banging. These forms are supposed to help us identify triggers. But because they are trapped on paper, spotting patterns across days or weeks is nearly impossible.

Later, these sheets are scanned into the patient's digital record for the Utilization Review team. This turns critical behavioral data into a static image. We are doing the work of data entry without gaining the intelligence of data analysis. We create a digital record that cannot be searched, audited, or analyzed.

I understand the hesitation to go fully digital. Hospitals have been hit hard by cyberattacks and ransomware. Maintaining manual redundancy is a valid business continuity strategy. But relying on paper for the primary workflow creates a different kind of risk: inaccurate data and missed clinical patterns. We are protecting against one failure mode by guaranteeing another.

This environment creates a tension you can feel on the floor. To be fair, we give patients significant autonomy. They keep their own clothes. They can pace in the hallway when it is not crowded. They get plenty of snacks. Yet some patients still say it "feels like jail." One patient told me they thought a staff member hated them. The impression came from nothing more than the staff member telling them to go back to their room while the hallway was crowded.

I wonder if it comes down to tone. Many staff have been here for years. Many have been assaulted. I believe they want to show empathy, but the circumstances grind it down. Patients get triggered easily by how something is said. I do not think anyone should lose their dignity just because they are admitted here. But I also know that some patients are extremely hard to redirect, and staff have to be firm. Even then, there is a better way. Staff should not yell at a patient or tell them to go back to their room without explaining why. There is usually a valid reason. We are doing shift change. We are handling an admission. We are responding to a code. Telling someone the reason takes five seconds and changes the entire interaction.

This tension is made worse by understaffing. We often have a census of up to 40 patients in one unit with only three nurses, usually one charge nurse, one med nurse, and one other, along with maybe five mental health workers. If we have multiple one-to-one observations or ligature watches running at the same time, the shortage becomes chronic. People are stretched too thin to be patient with patients.

Understaffing also shapes how we handle outbursts. I believe we default to Code Grey too quickly. We should give patients more time and try to de-escalate in a more coordinated way before ordering emergency intramuscular injections. I also do not think nurses and mental health workers should be the only ones de-escalating. We need a social worker or therapist present during crises. Most patients know their social worker determines their next step, so there is a built-in incentive for them to listen to that person.

I am conflicted about the chemical restraints. Some patients get hyper after receiving them. For some, it feels like they respond to the attention more than the medication, and the intervention loses its meaning. Often it is a miss, and we end up physically restraining them anyway. I have heard nurses talking about one patient who has not improved at all after months of medication changes. This patient has probably received more emergency injections than anyone on the unit, and their condition remains static. If these interventions are not working, something else needs to change.

The seclusion rooms bother me too. They should not be on the same unit where other patients can hear the screaming. That sound travels through the hall and raises the anxiety of everyone on the floor.

If the medications and the restraints and the seclusion are not producing results, we need better diagnostic tools. But maybe we also just need to listen more carefully. We need to pay attention to what patients are actually saying instead of dismissing it. A patient showed me some drawings once. Hidden in the corner of one sketch was a note. It said "They made me do this, I don't really want to. I just need help." I wanted to ask more questions. When I showed the nurse, they said the patient was just "buffing," which means faking it.

Sometimes the problems are simpler than we think. When I worked in the developmental disability space, a patient was agitated every morning. I finally asked them why. They told me their roommate did not let them sleep at night and the staff did not intervene.

Treating mental illness is incredibly difficult. I do not pretend otherwise. But there are glaring inefficiencies in how we do it here. I have already brought this up with the chief nursing officer. My goal is to help solve these problems, not just point them out. Stabilizing patients is not enough. We have to do more to improve their long-term recovery. I know that insurance is the biggest structural barrier, but inside these walls, we can do better.

We are managing risk. We are not yet healing people.
