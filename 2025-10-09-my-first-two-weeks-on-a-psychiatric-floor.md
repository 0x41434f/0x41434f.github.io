Today marks my first day off orientation and the end of my first two weeks as a mental health worker. Looking back, I realize how little I truly understood about psychiatry before stepping onto these floors. Now, I am certain that this is the most important work I can do in my lifetime.

My path here was not direct. It started last summer, when the life I had built came apart. In the middle of that collapse, I felt a strange calm, so unexpected that it sent me searching for answers. I read everything I could find about psychology and the brain, trying to understand my own response. That search led me to a core problem in psychiatry: there are still so few objective answers for what happens inside our minds.

That curiosity pushed me toward the clinic. I enrolled in a medical assistant program, where a simple exercise changed how I thought about tools. We were all taking blood pressure readings and struggling to distinguish the sounds through our stethoscopes. Everyone was getting different numbers. It made me wonder about the instruments we rely on. I started researching whether a better digital stethoscope could be built and found Eko Health, a company born from the exact same question. It confirmed something I had been circling around for months: my technical skills had a place in medicine.

From there, the path became clearer. I earned a certification as a Direct Support Professional and began working in a residential facility for adults with developmental disabilities and mental illness. Giving medications, documenting behaviors, seeing the direct impact of daily care on people's lives. That was where I knew I wanted to be a clinician. I applied to two psychiatric hospitals and landed here.

As a mental health worker, I am one of the people closest to the patients. We do the 15-minute observation rounds. We do the one-to-ones. We assist with therapeutic activities. I have also been involved in admissions and discharges. During admissions, I am often one of the first people to interact with a patient at their most vulnerable. I perform skin checks for contraband, take vitals, weigh and measure them, and inventory their personal belongings while the nurse assesses their condition. A huge amount of critical data, both clinical and personal, is gathered from the very first moment a patient walks in.

In my first two weeks, I have been floated across every unit, from the ICU to the specialized DDMI unit. That first day on the floor was a crystallizing moment. I saw people with such a wide range of behaviors and illnesses, and I knew I wanted to do more than observe and pass messages to the nurses. I wanted to build tools that could help solve their problems.

One memory from this week stays with me. A patient was in crisis, and when the staff could not de-escalate the situation, they called a code grey. I stood outside the seclusion room as they redirected the patient, and I started to cry. I did not have a relationship with this patient. It was my first time seeing them up close. I had seen redirections before without tearing up. I still do not fully understand my reaction. But I think it speaks to something about the weight of this work that I had not been prepared for, a weight I am now choosing to carry.

My experience here has already reinforced what I have learned in my research. I see the frustration in patients who wait days to see a psychiatrist for only a few minutes. I see nurses buried under administrative work and care coordination when patients assume they are only there to give shots and hand out medications. The need for better systems and better tools feels more urgent with every shift.

Over the next six months, my goal is to absorb as much as I can. I want to become a licensed psychiatric technician to take on more direct clinical responsibility, particularly in administering medication. That will deepen my understanding of psychiatric pharmacology and patient care as I continue on my path toward medical school. I will keep observing, documenting, and asking questions. Every observation is data. A patient's frustration with the system is data. My own unexpected tears outside a seclusion room are data. It is the human data that will guide me as I learn to build the tools that can support this work.
